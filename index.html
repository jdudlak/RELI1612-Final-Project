Issue One  ·  Five Sources

When
the CrossBecomesthe Brand

Four decades of hip hop fashion have transformed crosses, rosaries, and images of divine figures into luxury commodities — objects whose sacred meaning is simultaneously exploited and erased by market forces. This portfolio traces that transformation across five primary sources, asking who holds the authority to define the sacred, and what remains of religious meaning when it passes through the marketplace.

SOURCE 01 OF 05
Song

"My Adidas"

Run-D.M.C.  ·  Profile Records  ·  1986

What it is. A rap track dedicated entirely to the group's Adidas Superstar sneakers, released before any formal endorsement deal existed — making the song's devotion entirely organic, born from the streets of New York rather than a corporate boardroom.

Why it matters. "My Adidas" establishes the foundational logic of commodity-as-devotional-object in hip hop fashion. When Run-D.M.C. performed at Madison Square Garden and instructed 40,000 fans to raise their Adidas in the air, the concert became a congregation and the sneaker a sacred object. The same social mechanisms Miller identifies as the engines of religious meaning-making — repetition, communal performance, collective affirmation — were applied to a mass-produced consumer product. This stands as hip hop's first and most authentic brand partnership — the template upon which all subsequent relationships between hip hop and consumer goods, sacred or otherwise, would be built.

1986

"My Adidas walk through concert doors / And roam all over coliseum floors."

Run-D.M.C., 1986

The sneaker personified as companion — elevated to cultural symbol through communal ritual before any brand deal was signed. The devotion came before the deal.

SOURCE 02 OF 05
Song

"Hypnotize" &
The Jesus Piece

The Notorious B.I.G.  ·  Bad Boy Records  ·  1997

What it is. A posthumously released single in which Biggie references his diamond-encrusted Jesus pendant — a face of Christ in precious metals, commissioned from jeweler Tito Caicedo — worn alongside Cuban link chains as a marker of wealth and status.

Why it matters. The Jesus Piece marks the moment a traditionally sacred symbol enters hip hop fashion as a luxury status object. Biggie's lyric — "Cubans with the Jesus piece, with my peeps (Thank you, Lord)" — captures the dual register precisely: faith and fashion rendered inseparable by the rhyme scheme itself. As Beckford argues, what counts as religious is renegotiated through social processes — and in hip hop, those processes were increasingly commercial. The pendant was later adopted by Jay-Z, Travis Scott, and A$AP Rocky, many with no devotional relationship to Christianity, raising the portfolio's central question: does the wearer's intent determine the symbol's meaning, or does the symbol carry its sacred construction regardless?

Theoretical Frame
SACRED
AS
CONSTRUCT

Scholar Monica R. Miller, drawing on McCutcheon, argues that religion carries no inherent meaning outside of its social manufacturing — no sui generis essence that exists beyond cultural practice. Like race and gender, what we call sacred is real only insofar as dominant culture has agreed to treat it as such. Applied to hip hop fashion, this framework reveals commodification not as the destruction of religious meaning, but as a competing social process — one that re-manufactures sacred symbols within the logic of luxury, status, and brand identity.

SOURCE 03 OF 05
Performance & Merch Drop

Sunday Service — Coachella, Easter Sunday

Kanye West / Yeezy  ·  April 21, 2019

What it is. A gospel performance on a Coachella hillside on Easter Sunday, featuring a 100-person choir in custom Yeezy apparel — crewnecks reading "Holy Spirit," "Trust God" t-shirts, "Jesus Walks" socks, and choir ponchos — released as a limited merchandise drop alongside the performance.

Why it matters. Sunday Service represents the most deliberate blurring of personal faith and commercial spectacle in hip hop history. Kanye's deployment of religious imagery is grounded in a publicly documented Christian belief — making his commercialization distinct from artists who use sacred symbols purely aesthetically. McCutcheon's framework is visible in real time: religion being manufactured intentionally, its social construction on full display. Yet however genuine the faith behind it, the event simultaneously functioned as a brand activation and merchandise drop, raising the question of whether authenticity of belief can coexist with — or is ultimately consumed by — commercial intent.

Fans went further — founding "Yeezianity," an unofficial religion whose central tenet holds the artist as a divine model for human creative and moral behavior.

"He's a god. You're a god. Why Yeezus is the figure is because Kanye is the one who believes that the hardest."

— Yeezianity founder, via Vibe / Noisey, 2014

SOURCE 04 OF 05
Album & Merch Rollout

Whole
Lotta
Red

Playboi Carti  ·  Interscope  ·  December 25, 2020

What it is. Playboi Carti's second studio album, released on Christmas Day, accompanied by a merchandise drop featuring inverted cross motifs, gothic typography, and text including "Black Leather Devil" — framed by Carti's broader persona of satanic imagery, including an upside-down cross tattooed on his neck.

Why it matters. Carti's is the most theoretically revealing source in this portfolio. The inversion of the cross only functions as provocation because the cross retains enough socially constructed sacred meaning to be worth inverting — his entire aesthetic depends on the resilience of the religious construction he appears to reject. Commodification does not destroy sacred meaning; it exploits it. The Christmas Day release amplifies the provocation: a satanic-coded album on the most significant date in the Christian calendar. Miller's framework confirms what Carti makes visceral — a symbol built across centuries of collective practice cannot be neutralized by any single artist's commercial agenda.

2020

"The upside-down cross only provokes because the cross still means something."

Thesis — Portfolio Essay

Carti's anti-Christian aesthetic is as dependent on the sacred construction it opposes as Kanye's devotional branding is on the faith it expresses. Both confirm: the sacred and the commercial compete for the same symbols — and neither ever fully wins.

Istaghfar Allah
God, forgive me  ·  French Montana, April 2026

Instagram Story — Chrome Hearts Jeans

French Montana  ·  Instagram  ·  April 2026

What it is. A viral Instagram Story in which Muslim rapper French Montana posted a $200,000 pair of Chrome Hearts jeans — covered in gothic black and pink cross patches — with the caption: "200k for the jeans but istaghfar Allah I can't wear them lol."

Four decades of commercial machinery had so thoroughly absorbed the cross into the logic of luxury that its sacred construction became invisible — until, in a single moment of religious reckoning, it became the only thing that mattered.

Why it matters. This source introduces a non-Christian perspective that no other source in this portfolio provides. French Montana did not choose the crosses on those jeans as an act of devotion or rebellion — he did not register them as religious symbols at all until after the purchase. It is the portfolio's thesis made tangible: the commodity is powerful enough to render sacred meaning invisible — until, as French Montana demonstrated, it isn't.
